\[
x_{i}-x_{j}=\operatorname{lcm}(x_{1},x_{2}, \ldots, x_{m})+\cdots+\operatorname{lcm}(x_{1}, \ldots, x_{r})
\]
for some strictly increasing sequences of positive integers $m>r>n$. 

(D. Fon-Domain)

Solution:
Consider the problem to find all positive integers \( n \) for which there exists a set of distinct positive integers \( \{ x_i \}_{1 \leq i \leq n} \) satisfying:

\[
x_i - x_j = \text{lcm}(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_m) + \cdots + \text{lcm}(x_1, \ldots, x_r)
\]

for some strictly increasing sequences of positive integers \( m > r > n \).

To solve this, we first prove that if \( k \) is even then no such integer \( n \) exists, while if \( k \) is odd then all integers satisfy.

### Proof

1. **Base Case**: Consider \( n \in [k] / 2 \), and let \( f(x)=x-kx^{\frac{k}{2}} \). Suppose \( f(x_{i+1})=f(x_i)+b>0\). If \( f(x_{i+k}-x_{i})=0 \), by symmetry, \( f(x_{i+1+i/k})<f(x_{i}), \) which violates the strict increasing sequence condition. Thus, \( b<0\). Now, observe that:

   \[
   f(y)-f(x) \ge \lfloor \sqrt{y} \rfloor - \left\lfloor \sqrt{x} \right\rfloor.
   \]

   This inequality can be derived using calculus as follows: take partial derivatives of both sides with respect to \( y \) and \( x \):

   \[
   \begin{aligned}
   &\quad (\frac{d}{dy})f(y)-(\frac{d}{dx})f(x)=0 \\
   &\iff y-\sqrt{y}>x-\sqrt{x} > 0 \\
   &\iff y+x-y-x \\ &= (\frac{d}{dy})(\sqrt{y}+ \sqrt{x})-(\frac{d}{dx})(\sqrt{y}+\sqrt{x}) < (\sqrt{x}-\sqrt{y}).
   \end{aligned}
   \]

   Therefore,

   \[
   f(y)-f(x) \ge \lfloor \sqrt{y} \rfloor - \left\lfloor \sqrt{x} \right\rfloor,
   \]

   which contradicts our assumption since \( y>x \).

2. **Inductive Hypothesis**: Assume the result holds for any \( t<k \), and consider \( t+2 \) in the proof.

   By induction hypothesis, suppose we have \( |f(a)|<t\). Then, considering \( x_{i+1}=a^{k-1}\cdot(-1)^{(k-1)t}+(-1)^{(kt-t)}a^{kt}\) and \( x_{i}=-a^{t-1}+(-1)^{-t(t-1)}a^{t+1} \), where \( t \equiv k-1 \pmod{2} \), ensures that we maintain strict monotonicity. Then no two adjacent squares (horizontally or vertically) can be red because if they were not, we could add another square to the topmost row or leftmost column without violating this restriction, contradiction.

Therefore, these two squares have to share a vertex with another red square, and thus their shared vertex must also be a red square. But then our original condition gives two new constraints, implying that either there are fewer than five red squares (which is impossible since we already know there were four corners that were red), or there are less than seven total red squares. Hence, this cannot happen, and consequently, the red and blue squares form a perfect 7-by-7 checkerboard. The number of such configurations is $\binom{7}{2}=21$.

\binom{7}{2} = 21
