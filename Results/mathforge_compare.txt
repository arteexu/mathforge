Problem:
Find all triples $(a,b,c)$ of positive integers such that $a+b+c=20$ and $\operatorname{lcm}(a,b,c)=3^m \cdot n$ for some positive integer $m$ and positive integer $n$.

Solution:
By the fundamental theorem of arithemetic, we have $3$ divides at least one of $a,b,c$. If $a=3t$, then $\operatorname{lcm}(a,b,c) \geq 3^{2} \cdot t^{2}$, so $m \geq 2$. On the other hand, if $b=c=3t+1$, then $\operatorname{lcm}(a,b,c)=3^{3} \cdot(t+1)^{2}$; thus, $m=3$, and $n=(t+1)^{2}$. We also have $\operatorname{lcm}(a,b,c) \leq 20^{3}$. Therefore, $t \leq 4$, which means $n$ is at most $25$. However, $25$ does not divide any number of the form $(t+1)^{2}$, so there are no solutions in this case either. Thus, there are only two cases to consider:

Case 1: $a=3t$. Then, $b=c=6-t$, and we need $$(6-t)^{2} \leq 20^{3} \Longrightarrow 6-t \leq 40 \Longrightarrow t \geq 27$$ This gives us $t=28,29,30$, and hence the solutions are $(84,4,12),(90,2,18),(92,0,22)$.

Case 2: $b=3t+1$. Then, $a=c=6-t$, and we need $$(6-t)^{2} \leq 20^{3} \Longrightarrow 6-t \leq 40 \Longrightarrow t \geq -34$$ The only possible value for $t$ is $35$, so $b=103$, $a=c=5$, and we get the unique solution $(103,5,5)$.

It's clear that these are the only solutions. Indeed, it suffices to show that $\operatorname{lcm}(a,b,c)=3^{m} \cdot n$ implies that each of $a,b,c$ is divisible by $3$. Assume otherwise, and let $a=a^{\prime} / d, b=b^{\prime} / e, c=c^{\prime} / f$, where $d, e, f$ are pairwise relatively prime, $d e f=1$, and $3 \mid a^{\prime}, 3 \mid b^{\prime}, 3 \mid c^{\prime}$. This implies that $$\begin{aligned} 3^{\text {gcd }(\operatorname{ord}_{3}(d),\operatorname{ord}_{3}(e),\operatorname{ord}_{3}(f))} & =\operatorname{lcm}(a^{\prime}, b^{\prime}, c^{\prime}) \\ & =\frac{(a^{\prime})(b^{\prime})(c^{\prime})}{d e f} \\ & =\frac{a b c}{d e f} \\ & =3^{m} \end{aligned}$$ Therefore, $\operatorname{gcd}(\operatorname{ord}_{3}(d), \operatorname{ord}_{3}(e), \operatorname{ord}_{3}(f)) \leq m$. Since $\operatorname{gcd}(\operatorname{ord}_{3}(d), \operatorname{ord}_{3}(e), \operatorname{ord}_{3}(f))$ divides both $\operatorname{ord}_{3}(d)$ and $\operatorname{ord}_{3}(e)$, it must divide both $\operatorname{ord}_{3}(d e)$ and $\operatorname{ord}_{3}(f)$. But the order of $d e f$ can be arbitrarily large, since $\operatorname{ord}_{3}(d e f)=2 \operatorname{ord}_{3}(d)+\operatorname{ord}_{3}(e)-1$, which is clearly greater than $\operatorname{ord}_{3}(f)$. This contradiction proves the desired result.

Answer: $(84,4,12)$, $(90,2,18)$, $(92,0,22)$, $(103,5,5)$

Problem:
Compute $\sum_{k=1}^{100} \sum_{\substack{m+n=2k \\ k<m<n}} \frac{(2k-1)!!}{k!}$.

Solution:
We need to compute the sum:
\[
\sum_{k=1}^{100} \sum_{\substack{m+n=2k \\ k<m<n}} \frac{(2k-1)!!}{k!}.
\]
First, we rewrite the inner sum as:
\[
\sum_{\substack{m+n=2k \\ k<m<n}} \frac{(2k-1)!!}{k!} = \frac{(2k-1)!!}{k!} \sum_{m+k=n}^n 1.
\]
The inner sum is simply the number of solutions to $m+k=n$, which is 1. Thus, we have:
\[
\sum_{k=1}^{100} \frac{(2k-1)!!}{k!} = \sum_{k=1}^{100} \frac{(2k-1)!!}{k!} = \sum_{k=1}^{100} \frac{\prod_{j=1}^{2k-1} j}{k!} = \sum_{k=1}^{100} \frac{A(2k)}{k!},
\]
where $A(n)$ denotes the $n$th harmonic number. Therefore, our final answer is the $100$th harmonic number minus 1:
\[
H(100) - 1 = 100.
\]

Thus, the answer is $\boxed{100}$.

Answer: 100

**Problem Statement:**

How many ways are there to choose two non-consecutive pairs of vertices from a regular pentagon such that each pair is connected by an edge in the pentagon?

---

**Solution:**

Consider a regular pentagon inscribed in a circle. Let's label the vertices of the pentagon as \(A_1, A_2, A_3, A_4, A_5\) in clockwise order.

We need to count the number of ways to choose two non-consecutive pairs of vertices such that each pair is connected by an edge in the pentagon. Note that the only edges in the pentagon are the sides \(A_iA_{i+1}\) for \(i = 1, 2, 3, 4\).

First, let's consider the possible choices for the first pair \((A_i, A_j)\). Since we want the pairs to be non-consecutive, \(i\) and \(j\) must be adjacent or separated by at least one vertex. 

- If \(i = 1\), then \(j\) can be 2 or 4 (since \(j \neq i\)).
- If \(i = 2\), then \(j\) can be 3 or 5.
- If \(i = 3\), then \(j\) can be 4 or 1.
- If \(i = 4\), then \(j\) can be 5 or 2.
- If \(i = 5\), then \(j\) can be 1 or 3.

From these possibilities, we get the following valid pairs:
- From \(i = 1\), we have the pairs \((A_1, A_2)\) and \((A_1, A_4)\).
- From \(i = 2\), we have the pairs \((A_2, A_3)\) and \((A_2, A_5)\).
- From \(i = 3\), we have the pairs \((A_3, A_4)\) and \((A_3, A_1)\).
- From \(i = 4\), we have the pairs \((A_4, A_5)\) and \((A_4, A_2)\).
- From \(i = 5\), we have the pairs \((A_5, A_1)\) and \((A_5, A_3)\).

This gives us a total of \(6\) valid pairs from the first choice.
For each of these pairs, there are \(2\) ways to choose the second pair such that they are non-consecutive. Therefore, the total number of ways is \(6 \times 2 = 12\).

Thus, the number of ways to choose two non-consecutive pairs of vertices from a regular pentagon such that each pair is connected by an edge in the pentagon is \(\boxed{12}\).

Problem:
Let $a_{n}=n^{2}+2 n+2$. Determine the number of positive integers $k\leq 100$ such that $\frac{1}{a_{1}}+\frac{1}{a_{2}}+\cdots+\frac{1}{a_{k}} \geq \frac{k}{49}$.

Solution:
For any positive integer $k$, $$\frac{1}{a_{k}}=\frac{1}{(2 k+1)^{2}+2(2 k+1)+2}=\frac{(4 k+5)(k+1)}{(2 k+1)^{2}(k+1)}=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{(2 k+1)^{2}}-\frac{1}{(2 k+3)^{2}}+\frac{1}{(2 k+3)^{2}}-\frac{1}{(2 k+5)^{2}}+\frac{1}{(2 k+5)^{2}}-\frac{1}{(2 k+7)^{2}}+\ldots+\frac{1}{(2 k+5)^{2}}-\frac{1}{(2 k+7)^{2}}+\frac{1}{(2 k+7)^{2}}-\frac{1}{(2 k+9)^{2}}\right)$$ Since all terms except for the first and last are negative or zero, we get that $\frac{1}{a_{k}} \leq \frac{1}{(2 k+1)^{2}}-\frac{1}{(2 k+7)^{2}}$ and $\frac{1}{a_{k}} \geq \frac{1}{(2 k+3)^{2}}-\frac{1}{(2 k+5)^{2}}$. So $\frac{1}{a_{1}}+\frac{1}{a_{2}}+\cdots+\frac{1}{a_{k}} \geq \frac{1}{(2 k+1)^{2}}-\frac{1}{(2 k+7)^{2}}+\frac{1}{(2 k+3)^{2}}-\frac{1}{(2 k+5)^{2}}+\cdots+\frac{1}{(2 k+9)^{2}}-\frac{1}{(2 k+11)^{2}}= \frac{1}{(2 k+1)^{2}}-\frac{1}{(2 k+11)^{2}}$ By induction on $k$, we see that $\frac{1}{(2 k+1)^{2}}-\frac{1}{(2 k+11)^{2}}=\frac{8 k}{(2 k+1)(2 k+11)} \geq \frac{k}{49}$, as desired.

Answer: 23

We need to find \( x \) such that \( x \times 27 \leq M < (x + 1) \times 27 \).

Let's denote \( x = 100000000 + n \) where \( n \) is a 9-digit number. Then,
\[ 100000000 + n \times 27 \leq 27 \times (100000000 + n) < (100000000 + n + 1) \times 27. \]

Simplifying the inequality:
\[ 27 \times 100000000 + 27n \leq 27 \times (100000000 + n) < 27 \times (100000000 + n + 1). \]
This reduces to:
\[ 27n \leq 27n \leq 27n + 27. \]

By subtracting \( 27n \) from all sides, we get:
\[ 0 \leq 27 \leq 27. \]

The only way this can hold true is if \( x = 1999999999 \). Let's check:
\[ 1999999999 \times 27 = 5399999993 \]
and
\[ 27 \times 1999999999 = 5399999993. \]

Therefore, \( x = 1999999999 \).

So, the final answer is \( x = 1999999999 \).
